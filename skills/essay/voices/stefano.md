## Distilled Voice Profile

**Sentence rhythm**
- Alternates long, clause-stacked sentences (setting up context, piling on subordinate clauses) with short, blunt one-liners used as punches: "That's a problem." / "I think such a gut reaction is a mistake." / "We thought open source won. The rush of victory didn't last long."
- Frequently opens a section with a short declarative sentence, then complicates or qualifies it over the next few sentences.
- Rhetorical questions used to pivot a section or close it: "Will it be a tool controlled by a few, or a shared resource for all?"

**Vocabulary / word choice**
- Domain-fluent but not jargon-heavy — explains technical/legal terms (SCCs, GPLv3, FISA) in plain language right after naming them.
- Reaches for vivid, slightly irreverent metaphors: "Trojan horse," "toll booths," "a club of clouds," "the loot," "zombie users," "nuclear power plant to train new models."
- Comfortable with dry sarcasm and understatement: "The Italian government is known for many things – porn-star politicians, a revolving door of power, huge public debt – and, yeah, most of them aren't good."
- Uses first person freely and directly states personal opinion/uncertainty: "I think," "I fear," "My hope is," "I'm not too surprised."

**Paragraph structure**
- Short paragraphs, often 2-4 sentences; rarely a long unbroken block.
- Uses bolded lead-in phrases or bullet lists to break up argumentative sections (e.g. "Get involved," workshop participant lists).
- Section headers (H2/H3) used to segment a clear argumentative arc: sets up a problem, complicates it, proposes a path forward.

**Transitions**
- Avoids mechanical connectors ("furthermore," "in conclusion"). Prefers abrupt, conversational pivots: "But I'm not popping that jig just yet." / "Now guess who would suffer..." / "Don't get me wrong:"
- Often transitions by directly addressing the reader or posing a question rather than a linking clause.

**Recurring tics / phrases**
- "I think" / "I fear" / "My hope is" as opinion markers.
- Address to the reader in second person for emphasis: "Are you reading this on a mobile phone?"
- Uses em dashes and parentheticals liberally for asides, often with a wry aside inside them.
- Ends pieces on a call to action or a pointed rhetorical question rather than a neat summary.
- Self-deprecating or personally-implicated framing ("I fought Microsoft," "I sighed in disappointment," "That's where I dropped too").

**Argument shape**
- Classic arc: personal anecdote or concrete example → broader structural/systemic diagnosis → named tension between two forces (open vs. proprietary, individual vs. corporation, principle vs. pragmatism) → call to action or open question, rarely a tidy resolution.
- Willing to hold an uncomfortable, nuanced position explicitly against a more popular gut-reaction take (e.g. "theft" piece, Stallman piece) — states the popular view, validates the underlying feeling, then argues against the conclusion.

**Things to avoid (anti-patterns)**
- Don't flatten this into generic "passionate advocate" tone — the specificity is in the concrete historical detail (dates, named people, named organizations, dollar figures) used to ground abstract policy arguments.
- Don't smooth out the sarcasm/wryness into neutral corporate tone.
- Don't over-hedge — this voice states opinions plainly ("I think X is wrong") rather than "some might argue."
- Don't lose the first-person stake in the topic — he writes as a participant in these fights (FSF Italy chapter, OSI executive director), not a neutral observer.
