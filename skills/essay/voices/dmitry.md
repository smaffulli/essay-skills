## Distilled Voice Profile

**Sentence rhythm**
- Builds through short, plain declaratives, then punctuates with a one-line gut-check: "This is bad for a spreadsheet." / "No, in fact, this code was buggy." / "That's not good enough."
- Frequently narrates in real time, as if thinking aloud alongside the reader: "Let's think why this happened?" / "We should check." / "OK, done yet?"
- Uses direct address and mock dialogue with the reader to build suspense before a reveal: "Right?" / "No, don't tell me." / "Whew, you sigh!"

**Vocabulary / word choice**
- Precise and technical when precision matters (transitivity, anti-symmetric, reflexive, OSI-approved), but always defines the term in plain language immediately after using it.
- Otherwise plain, unpretentious word choice — avoids business jargon even when writing about business models.
- Occasional deadpan understatement and self-aware asides: "sorting continues to surprise me with delightful bugs. Frustrating too, but also delightful."
- Repeats a signature rhetorical device: state a comforting claim, then undercut it — "And yet this is a bit of a lie." (used almost verbatim in both the sorting piece and the sovereign lumber piece).

**Paragraph structure**
- Short paragraphs, frequently one or two sentences.
- Heavy use of code blocks / bullet lists / numbered lists to carry the argument's actual evidence, with prose in between narrating what just happened and why it matters.
- Section headers mark stages of a journey ("Comparators" → "Transitivity" → "But why is sort so fickle?" → "I will have order!" → "OK, done yet?" → "In conclusion") — headers read like plot beats, not topic labels.

**Transitions**
- Moves between sections by posing a question the next section answers, not by linking phrases.
- Often opens a new section by immediately contradicting or complicating the previous one's tentative conclusion ("This looks correct... Right? No, this would be too easy.").
- Uses "Well," "So," "But," "Now" as light conversational connectors rather than formal transitions.

**Recurring tics / phrases**
- "And yet this is a bit of a lie." — signature move for puncturing a comforting simplification.
- Rhetorical "Right?" / "Doh!" / "No, don't tell me." as asides that mimic the reader's internal reaction.
- Analogies carried through an entire piece rather than used once (lumber/chairs sustained across the full "Sovereign Lumber" essay).
- States personal history/credentials briefly and self-deprecatingly when relevant ("graduate-level courses... from Michael Rabin," "I remember learning them in AP Computer Science") rather than as authority-flexing.
- Ends pieces by inviting the reader into unresolved territory rather than a tidy wrap-up: "I'd love to hear from you... Please share your thoughts!" / "If your values are a single type... all these lessons apply."

**Argument shape**
- Pedagogical, iterative structure: try an obvious/naive solution → show exactly how and why it fails with a concrete example → refine → try again → still incomplete → refine again → land on a general principle.
- For opinion/policy pieces (Sovereign Lumber): borrow someone else's framework or analogy, test it against a real case, find where it strains, then propose a refined version as a numbered list of properties/requirements.
- Comfortable ending without a fully resolved answer — flags open questions explicitly rather than forcing a neat conclusion.

**Things to avoid (anti-patterns)**
- Don't smooth away the "thinking out loud" quality into a polished, pre-digested explanation — the false starts and wrong turns are the point, not filler to cut.
- Don't drop the concrete code/example-driven grounding for abstract claims — every claim gets a minimal reproducible example (a code snippet, a numbered property, a named real-world case).
- Don't oversell confidence — this voice explicitly flags uncertainty ("I know just enough economics to know nothing of this sort can work") rather than asserting.
- Don't lose the collaborative, community-facing close — pieces read as an invitation to a shared problem, not a lecture.
